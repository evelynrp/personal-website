When I saw the first Sonic movie, it was out of morbid curiosity. Surely there was no way the studio could have salvaged what was sure to be a trainwreck, another cash-grab live action film preying on nostalgia with no meaningful substance to speak of.

Boy, was I wrong.

The first movie wasn't perfect. It had a few too many dated jokes and Fortnite dances. It was a little generic. But it wasn't remotely *bad*. It was, in fact, a somewhat touching tale about a blue alien finding a family in a faraway land, and learning to stop running (lol) and stand up to protect what was important to him. Classic shonen manga stuff.

*Sonic 2* built on this and reached new heights. While still not perfect, with some minor pacing issues, it reached new levels of emotional resonance and cinematic fight scene special effects glory.

While *Sonic 2* succeeded with powerful emotional beats via a disciplined and solid three-act structure following the Hero's Journey, *Sonic 3* is held back by its lack of focus and prioritization of spectacle and fanservice over substantive narrative and meaningful character development.

#### Dragon Balls

*Sonic* and *Dragon Ball* are frequently compared, with good reason. In *Dragon Ball*, there are seven magical Dragon Balls which, when brought together, summon the mystical Eternal Dragon Shenron who is capable of granting and wish; once the wish is made, the Dragon disappears and the Dragon Balls are scattered across the earth and must be gathered again. Also, Goku has a super form in which his hair turns yellow and his power level skyrockets.

In *Sonic*, at least in the games, there are seven Chaos Emeralds which, when brought together, gives Sonic access to a super form in which his hair turns yellow, his power level skyrockets, and he gains the power of flight. When he leaves this form, the Chaos Emeralds are scattered across the earth and must be gathered again.

The movies take a different approach to the Chaos Emeralds. The Chaos Emeralds barely exist, making an unnamed cameo after the Master Emerald is destroyed at the end of *Sonic 2* where it is revealed that the Master Emerald is somehow made up of seven smaller emeralds. Rather than being scattered after use, the Master Emerald is simply always there and always an option for the heroes to use. There are no stakes to using the Master Emerald, since it does not have to be rebuilt or recharged, aside from the vague moral obligation the heroes have to resist ultimate power, despite Sonic having already proven quite conclusively at the end of *Sonic 2* that he cares significantly more about chili dogs than he does about power.

*Dragon Ball* recognizes the problem of having an all-powerful wish-granting MacGuffin needing appropriate stakes. As the series progresses, the combination of the Dragon Radar, a device which can detect the Dragon Balls' magical signature, and the heroes' increasing power creep make gathering the Dragon Balls a relatively trivial task. More than once the heroes use the Dragon Balls to restore life the thousands, or even to restore the entire Earth after it was destroyed. *Dragon Ball GT* handles this by introducing the Black Dragon Balls, a shadow version of the Dragon Balls which are a manifestation of the evil inherent in the wishes Shenron grants. These Black Dragon Balls are scattered throughout the universe rather than simply across the Earth, and must be returned to Earth to prevent its complete destruction. Following the recent revival of the main *Dragon Ball* series, this is mirrored by the introduction of some sort of evil Black Goku or Black Gohan or something.

The Sonic films do nothing of the sort, or at least not yet. Despite Sonic's promise in *Sonic 3* Act 2 Scene 6 not to use the Master Emerald, he uses the Emerald anyway with no consequences whatsoever. There are no stakes and no emotional or narrative meaning to the decision not to use the Emerald earlier in the film.

## Jim Carrey

This section is likely to be divisive if the reviews on RottenTomatoes are any indication of public opinion, but Jim Carrey's performance in *Sonic 3* does the film a serious disservice, especially when compared to his performance in the previous film. Rather than contributing to the plot in any meaningful way, or at least being funny, many of Carrey's scenes in *Sonic 3* are senseless. This is a huge disappointment after his tremendous showing in the previous films.

On a personal note, I was not Carrey's biggest fan before *Sonic 1*, but cannot imagine anybody else fitting the role so well. He was never going to be a video-game-accurate portrayal, since Eggman is such a cartoonish character, but the choice to make him an eccentric, antisocial genius coupled perfectly with Carrey's physical comedy and facial exaggerations. This was adaptation in its highest form: abandoning what could never work in the new medium while telling a complete and satisfying story which captures the tone and spirit of the original. This is what made Carrey's bizarre role in *Sonic 3* so tragic.

### Dancing

Both *Sonic 2* and *Sonic 3* have long dance scenes. In *Sonic 2*, the dance scene occurs in some sort of Russian pub at which Sonic and Tails stop on their quest to find the Master Macguffin. In *Sonic 3*, the dance scene occurs as the Robotniks are en route to the secret key to the not-Space-Colony-ARK underneath a highly-secure GUN facility for some reason(?). Admittedly, both plots are somewhat contrived, but that's not what this section is about.
